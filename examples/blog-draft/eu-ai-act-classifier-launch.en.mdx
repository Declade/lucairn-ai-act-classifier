# We Built a Free CLI That Maps Any AI Use Case to the EU AI Act Articles It Triggers. Here's What 50 Real Cases Taught Us.

The EU AI Act is 113 articles, 13 annexes, and 144 pages of dense regulatory prose. Mapping a single concrete AI use case ("we use a model to rank job applicants by CV") to the specific articles, paragraphs, and sub-letters it triggers takes a competent compliance practitioner somewhere between 6 and 10 hours. We measured this on three of the use cases below before we started shipping the classifier.

We did not enjoy the 6-10 hours per use case. Neither will the next compliance team to face the same question. So we built a tool.

## The problem

There is a structural mismatch between how the EU AI Act is written and how product teams build AI systems.

The Act is structured top-down by regulator concern: prohibited practices in Article 5, high-risk classification in Article 6 plus Annex III, technical obligations cascading through Articles 10-15, transparency obligations in Article 50, governance in Articles 70+, and so on. Each paragraph references other paragraphs. Each annex paragraph references the article that invokes it. Reading the regulation linearly produces a map of *the regulator's mental model*.

Product teams approach the problem bottom-up. They have a concrete use case: "we screen CVs," "we forecast weather," "we generate deepfakes." They want to know, for that one use case, which articles apply, what evidence the regulator will eventually ask for, and what the timeline is for getting compliant.

The shortest path between those two reading orders is hours of cross-referencing. Compliance counsel charge for that hour. Internal counsel does not exist at most early-stage AI companies. The result is a real, observable industry failure mode: companies either over-classify (assume everything is high-risk; pay for AI-Act compliance theatre against use cases that don't even fall under Annex III) or under-classify (assume Article 5 isn't about them; ship a product that triggers a prohibition they didn't realise existed until the regulator reaches out).

Builders do not read regulation. Lawyers do not read architecture diagrams. Someone has to bridge the gap, repeatedly, for every new AI use case. Today, that someone is overwhelmingly a senior compliance consultant whose hourly rate makes the exercise economically prohibitive below a certain product size.

## What the tool does

`@lucairn/ai-act-classifier` is a single command. You give it a free-text description of an AI use case. It returns the specific Annex III paragraph(s), Article 5 prohibition letter(s), Article 50 paragraph(s), and the resulting Articles 10/12/13/14/15 cascade applicability. It works in English and German. It runs entirely on your machine in deterministic mode (no network call, no LLM, no data leaving your laptop). It is free, MIT-licensed, and installable from npm.

```bash
$ npx @lucairn/ai-act-classifier "Our AI tool screens CVs and ranks job applicants based on resume content and predicted job-fit scores; HR teams use the candidate ranking for hiring decisions."
```

Real output:

```
  EU AI Act mapping
  Article 5  (prohibited): not triggered
  Article 6  + Annex III:      HIGH-RISK
    └─ Annex III.4(a) — Employment, workers' management and access to self-employment
  Article 10 (data governance): applies
  Article 12 (logging/record-keeping): applies
  Article 13 (deployer transparency): applies
  Article 14 (human oversight): applies
  Article 15 (accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity): applies
  Article 50 (GPAI/deployer):  not triggered
  Annex IV  (technical docs):  REQUIRED

  Lucairn obligation overlay
  Cat 1 Sanitizer  (Art 10+15):  REQUIRED
  Cat 2 Evidence   (Art 12+14):  REQUIRED
  Cat 3 Inventory  (Art 10+12+14+15): REQUIRED

  Confidence: 0.56   Mode: deterministic   Rules: v0.1.1 (sha256:12eafb8a…)

  ⚠ Informational tool — not legal advice. See README §Disclaimer.
```

Add `--explain` to get a reasoning trace with verbatim EUR-Lex paragraph chapeaux, matched lexicon phrases, sub-letter narrowing branch, and a citation URL for every fired article. Add `--lang de` for German. Add `--llm anthropic` (or `openai` or `groq`) to swap the deterministic keyword extractor for an opt-in LLM call against your own API key, when the use case description does not match the curated lexicon's canonical phrases.

The output is consultant-paste-friendly markdown by default, parseable JSON via `--format json`, or terminal-pipe-friendly plain text via `--explain-format text`. There is a `--cite` flag that emits the EUR-Lex + EU AI Office Service Desk + lucairn.eu citation block as a copy-paste block for a compliance memo footer. There is an `--annex iv` flag that prints the Annex IV technical-documentation 9-item reference table. There is a `--rules-version` flag that asserts the loaded rules match a version pin (useful in CI to catch surprise classifier upgrades).

## The locked three-category obligation mapping

We ship one opinionated overlay alongside the neutral article mapping: the Lucairn three-category framing.

It works like this. The high-risk obligation surface in the EU AI Act is articles 10 + 12 + 13 + 14 + 15. Reading those articles plus the corresponding implementing acts plus the harmonised standards plus the BSI / BfDI / Bitkom guidance, three operational categories of work emerge. Not three new regulator categories — three groupings of the existing obligations, mapped to the three actual engineering workstreams a compliance-conscious team has to run:

- **Category 1 — Sanitizer (Articles 10 + 15).** Data and data governance plus accuracy / robustness / cybersecurity. This is everything that touches the training-data pipeline, the input/output filtering layer, the adversarial-robustness story.
- **Category 2 — Evidence (Articles 12 + 14).** Record-keeping plus human oversight. This is everything that produces audit trails, decision logs, oversight-trigger documentation, the evidence chain a regulator will ask for first.
- **Category 3 — Inventory (Articles 10 + 12 + 14 + 15).** The end-to-end "AI-system register" view — the inventory of which systems are deployed, who their oversight officer is, what data they trained on, what their logging surface looks like. This is the documentation product that organisationally lives across product + DPO + compliance + security.

The reason we ship this opinionated mapping rather than only the neutral article list is that the hedging-as-deliverable failure mode is too common. Compliance practitioners who classify but don't operationalise produce 50-page memos that say "Article 12 applies, you'll need record-keeping" without ever specifying what record-keeping infrastructure costs, who owns it, or when to start building. The three-category overlay forces the conversation into three concrete project plans, each with an owner, a budget, and a delivery date.

You can suppress the overlay with `--no-three-category` if you only want the regulator's view. We default-on it because the people most likely to use a classifier are also the people most likely to need to ship something.

## Five worked examples

The following five examples cover the load-bearing cases. Each output is real `dist/cli.js` output from the v0.1.1 binary, not an illustration.

### Example 1 — Employment screening (Annex III paragraph 4)

```
$ ai-act-classify "Our AI tool screens CVs and ranks job applicants based on resume content and predicted job-fit scores; HR teams use the candidate ranking for hiring decisions."

  Article 6  + Annex III:      HIGH-RISK
    └─ Annex III.4(a) — Employment, workers' management and access to self-employment
  Articles 10-15: all apply (cascade)
  Annex IV  (technical docs):  REQUIRED
```

The classifier fires Annex III paragraph 4(a) on the matched phrases `candidate ranking` and `hiring decisions`. The sub-letter narrowing routes to `(a)` (recruitment-and-selection) rather than `(b)` (workplace decisions) or `(c)` (workforce monitoring) because the input describes hiring-stage selection, not in-role evaluation. The full Articles 10-15 cascade then fires deterministically.

### Example 2 — Real-time biometric ID for law enforcement (Article 5 prohibition)

```
$ ai-act-classify "We deploy a real-time facial recognition system in public spaces for general law-enforcement surveillance."

  Article 5  (prohibited): PROHIBITED
  Article 6  + Annex III:      suppressed by Art 5
  Articles 10-15: all not applicable
```

This is Article 5(1)(h): the "use of real-time remote biometric identification systems in publicly accessible spaces for the purpose of law enforcement" prohibition. The classifier suppresses the Annex III high-risk classification — the prohibition pre-empts the obligation surface entirely. The output exits with status code 1 (Article 5 prohibition triggered) so CI pipelines can fail-loud on prohibited use cases.

### Example 3 — GPAI fine-tuning for a customer-service chatbot (Article 50 transparency)

```
$ ai-act-classify "We fine-tune Llama for our customer-service chatbot"

  Article 5  (prohibited): not triggered
  Article 6  + Annex III:      not triggered
  Article 50 (GPAI/deployer):  applies
  Annex IV  (technical docs):  not applicable
```

This is the Article 50(1) chatbot-disclosure path. Critically, the classifier does NOT fire Annex III — fine-tuning a general-purpose model for customer-service routing does not trigger any Annex III paragraph. The output is the limited-risk path: Article 50 transparency obligations only, no Annex IV technical documentation, no Articles 10-15 cascade.

### Example 4 — Deepfake content for public-interest news reporting (Article 50(4))

```
$ ai-act-classify "AI that generates synthetic deepfake video content for news articles on matters of public interest"

  Article 50 (GPAI/deployer):  applies
```

The classifier fires Article 50(4) sub-paragraph 1 on the matched `deepfake` phrase. The deployer obligation kicks in: AI-generated image, audio, or video content constituting a deep fake must be disclosed as artificially generated or manipulated. Article 50(4) sub-paragraph 1 has its own carve-out — when the content is part of an "evidently artistic, creative, satirical, fictional or analogous work or programme", the obligation narrows to disclosure that does not hamper enjoyment of the work. Article 50(4) sub-paragraph 2 is a separate trigger covering AI-generated TEXT published to inform the public on matters of public interest; its editorial-review carve-out applies to the accompanying news article text, not to the deepfake video itself. The classifier emits the sub-1 chapeau because the input describes video, not text.

### Example 5 — Negative case: product recommendations (no obligation fires)

```
$ ai-act-classify "AI that recommends products based on browsing history"

  Article 5  (prohibited): not triggered
  Article 6  + Annex III:      not triggered
  Article 50 (GPAI/deployer):  not triggered
```

Browsing-history-based product recommendation is the kind of mid-stakes consumer AI that often gets over-classified by compliance teams operating from first principles. The classifier correctly returns the empty obligation surface. The use case is regulated only by GDPR (consent + purpose-limitation + transparency) and the e-Commerce Directive transparency rules — neither of which is in scope for the AI Act classifier.

## What the 50 test cases revealed

The classifier ships with a 50-case curated fixture corpus at `test/fixtures/use-cases/`. The corpus covers 17 Annex III high-risk cases (every paragraph 1-8 with at least one sub-letter narrowing test), 7 Article 5 prohibitions (every letter a-i exercised), 8 Article 50 transparency cases (both deployer and provider sides), 9 negative cases (deliberately innocuous AI to test for false-positive over-firing), and 9 legacy cases retained from the Day-3 prototype.

The empirical numbers from `accuracy/REPORT.md`, regenerated on the v0.1.1 main branch:

- **Overall accuracy:** 98.2% (granular per-field pass rate across all 50 fixtures).
- **Article 5 prohibition detection:** 100.0% (every prohibition fires correctly; no false-positive prohibition flag against the 9 negative cases).
- **Binary high-risk classification:** 98.0% (Annex III plus Article 6, with sub-letter narrowing).

The honest framing: these numbers measure internal consistency between a curated fixture corpus and the lexicon. The corpus was shaped during Day 7-8 of the build to exercise the lexicon's canonical phrases. The numbers are not a measure of arbitrary-real-world accuracy. They are a measure of "if we agree on the canonical phrasing of a use case, the classifier maps it to the correct article surface." The corpus design discipline (every fixture cites a verbatim EUR-Lex paragraph; every fixture passes a regulator-validator review chain) makes the 98.2% number more honest than a higher number on an undisclosed corpus would be.

The opt-in LLM mode (`--llm anthropic`) was measured at 93.5-97.6% accuracy across two independent runs on the same 50-case corpus (Claude Haiku 4.5 model; deterministic mode is byte-stable across runs, LLM mode is probabilistic and varies). For reproducible classification, use deterministic mode. The LLM mode trades reproducibility for better coverage of paraphrased inputs that don't match the lexicon's canonical phrases.

What the 50 cases revealed during the build that we did not expect: a measurable fraction of the use cases that compliance teams would describe as "operational" or "logistical" actually trigger Annex III high-risk. Three concrete examples from the corpus:

- **Fixture-09 (Mitarbeiterüberwachung in der Logistikzentrale).** Worker monitoring in a logistics centre, paired with individual task allocation. Reads like operations work. Triggers Annex III paragraph 4(b) (decisions affecting terms of work-related relationships including promotion and termination, task allocation based on individual behaviour or personal traits or characteristics, or monitoring and evaluation of performance and behaviour of persons in such employment relationships) — full Articles 10-15 cascade.
- **Fixture-11 (Notrufdisposition / Rettungsleitstelle).** AI-assisted triage in an emergency-call dispatcher. Reads like humanitarian work. Triggers Annex III paragraph 5(d) (AI systems intended to evaluate and classify emergency calls by natural persons or to dispatch, or to establish priority in the dispatching of, emergency first response services — police, firefighters, medical aid — as well as emergency healthcare patient-triage systems).
- **Fixture-17 (voter targeting and election influencing).** Voter-targeting AI marketed for election influencing campaigns. Reads like marketing technology. Triggers Annex III paragraph 8(b) — election-influencing AI systems are high-risk regardless of whether the deployer self-describes the activity as marketing or as political work.

Each of these is the kind of use case where a self-classification first pass would routinely return "not high-risk." The classifier surfaces them as high-risk in roughly two seconds. That gap — between the practitioner's intuition and the regulator's framing — is what makes the tool useful.

## Limitations

The classifier will not replace your DPO. It will not replace your counsel. It will not, despite the temptation to phrase it that way in marketing copy, give legal advice.

What it gives you is a starting point: article numbers, paragraph references, the cascade applicability, and a citation chain. Everything downstream of that starting point — the deployer-vs-provider distinction, the harmonised-standard alignment, the cross-border scoping, the timeline impact of Article 113 staggered applicability dates, the interaction with sectoral law (medical devices, financial services, aviation) — needs human judgment.

The classifier knows about one residual misclassification, documented openly in `accuracy/KNOWN-MISCLASSIFICATIONS.md`. Fixture-28 (a German-language Article 50 emotion-marketing case) contains the compound noun `Emotionserkennungssystems`. The lexicon's 1-gram `emotionserkennung` does not fire inside the compound noun because the extractor tokenises on whitespace and punctuation, not on compound-noun-internal morphological roots. The classifier correctly fires Article 50(3) but misses the parallel Annex III paragraph 1(c) emotion-recognition high-risk classification. We document this rather than engineer around it because honest disclosure of a known limitation beats a fake 100% accuracy claim. The fix is a hand-curated DE compound-noun split table; it is the top item in the v0.2 backlog.

The lexicon is hand-curated. It will miss paraphrases that humans recognise as semantically identical to the lexicon's canonical phrases. The opt-in `--llm` mode is the workaround when this happens. We tested three providers (Anthropic Haiku 4.5, OpenAI GPT-4o-mini, Groq Llama 3.3 70B) and ship them all. The LLM is constrained to cite phrases from the curated lexicon; any hallucinated phrase is dropped before the rules engine sees it.

## Use it. Fork it. Break it.

The tool is on npm. Install from `pnpm add @lucairn/ai-act-classifier` or run via `npx -y @lucairn/ai-act-classifier "<use case>"`. The source is at [github.com/Declade/lucairn-ai-act-classifier](https://github.com/Declade/lucairn-ai-act-classifier) under MIT for the code and CC-BY-4.0 for the curated test corpus.

If you find a classification you believe is legally wrong, open a GitHub issue with the input text, your expected output, the EUR-Lex paragraph that supports your reading, and (optionally) a fix proposal. We will treat such issues as launch-blockers for the next release.

If you are an EU/DE consultant who wants to cite the tool in client work, the `--cite` flag emits a Tier-1 + Tier-2 + Tier-3 citation block (EUR-Lex + EU AI Office Service Desk + lucairn.eu) you can paste into the memo footer.

The classifier is part of a bigger Lucairn compliance-tooling story. The companion tools `/tools/ai-payload-inspector`, `/tools/evidence-readiness`, and `/tools/mcp-risk-scanner` already live on lucairn.eu. The longer-form pieces on the regulation — [the EU AI Act architecture post](https://lucairn.eu/en/blog/eu-ai-act-architecture-2026), [the Article 12 logging guidance](https://lucairn.eu/en/blog/ai-act-article-12-logging-in-practice), [the Article 50 GPAI deployer brief](https://lucairn.eu/en/blog/article-50-gpai-deployers-2026) — sit alongside the classifier as part of the same project.

This is informational tooling. Not legal advice. Not a substitute for counsel. But the article-number-mapping question is now a `npx` away, and the 6-10 hours it used to take can go into the obligations actually downstream of the classification.

That's the trade we wanted. We hope it's useful.
